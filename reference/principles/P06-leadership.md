# P06 — Demonstrate Leadership Behaviors
**Type:** Principle
**PMBOK8 Source:** PMBOK8 Standard §3.6 — Demonstrate Leadership Behaviors
**Version:** 1.0.0

## Definition
PMBOK 8 Standard §3.6 establishes that effective project delivery requires leadership behaviors at all levels of the project — not only from the project manager or those in formal authority positions. Leadership is fundamentally distinct from authority: it is the capacity to influence, inspire, and guide others toward shared goals through behavior, not through positional power. Leadership behaviors include motivating and inspiring team members, communicating with clarity and purpose, modeling the behaviors the project requires, creating the conditions for team success, managing conflict constructively, facilitating decision-making, and accepting accountability for outcomes. Effective leaders adapt their style to the context, the individuals involved, and the nature of the work — and they develop leadership capacity in others as a deliberate act of stewardship.

## Key Characteristics
- **Leadership distinct from authority:** Leadership influence is earned through demonstrated competence, integrity, and care — it does not automatically accompany a project management title or organizational position (§3.6).
- **Distributed leadership:** Leadership behaviors are needed at all levels of the project team and can be demonstrated by any team member — not reserved for the project manager or sponsor (§3.6).
- **Clarity and purposeful communication:** Effective leaders communicate in ways that reduce ambiguity, align the team on priorities, and help individuals understand how their work connects to the project's purpose (§3.6).
- **Conflict management:** Leaders address conflict constructively — neither ignoring it nor escalating unnecessarily — recognizing that managed conflict can strengthen team relationships and improve decision quality (§3.6).
- **Adaptive leadership style:** Effective leaders adapt their style — directive, coaching, facilitative, or supportive — based on the individual's needs, the team's development stage, and the situational context (§3.6).
- **Modeling desired behaviors:** Leaders create the behavioral norms of the project by what they do, not only what they say — demonstrating accountability, transparency, respect, and commitment that they expect from others (§3.6).

## Questions to Ask in Practice
1. Am I influencing this project's outcomes through demonstrated leadership behaviors — or am I relying primarily on positional authority, which may not be effective when I most need it?
2. Are there team members who are not in formal authority positions but are demonstrating strong leadership — and am I creating space for them to lead where it adds value?
3. How am I adapting my leadership style to the individual needs of different team members — and to the evolving situation of the project?
4. When conflict arises in the team or with stakeholders, am I addressing it constructively and promptly — or allowing it to fester in ways that damage relationships and project performance?
5. What behaviors am I modeling that the team is likely to replicate — and are those the behaviors that this project needs to succeed?
