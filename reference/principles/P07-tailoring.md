# P07 — Tailor Based on Context
**Type:** Principle
**PMBOK8 Source:** PMBOK8 Standard §3.7 — Tailor Based on Context
**Version:** 1.0.0

## Definition
Tailoring, as defined in PMBOK 8 Standard §3.7, is the deliberate adaptation of the project approach — including processes, methods, templates, tools, and governance structures — to fit the unique context of each project. No single methodology or process framework is appropriate for all situations. The tailoring practitioner evaluates project characteristics (size, complexity, novelty, strategic importance, team capability, regulatory environment, stakeholder needs, and delivery cadence) and makes conscious choices about what to apply, what to modify, and what to omit. Both over-application and under-application of standards are recognized as risks. Tailoring is not a one-time decision at project initiation — it is an ongoing, reflective practice.

## Key Characteristics
- **Context-driven selection:** Every tailoring decision flows from an honest assessment of project context, including organizational culture, industry constraints, team maturity, and risk profile (§3.7).
- **Proportionality:** Governance and documentation effort must be proportionate to the project's size, strategic significance, and complexity — heavier governance for complex, high-risk work; lighter governance for routine, low-risk work (§3.7).
- **Ongoing reassessment:** Tailoring is not finalized at project start; as context evolves (scope changes, team changes, regulatory updates), the approach is revisited and adapted (§3.7).
- **Value orientation:** The purpose of tailoring is to increase the likelihood of value delivery — processes are selected or omitted based on whether they add value in the given context, not because they appear in a standard (§3.7).
- **Integration with delivery approach:** Tailoring determines the appropriate balance between predictive, iterative, incremental, agile, and hybrid delivery models for the specific project (§3.7).
- **Organizational enablement:** Effective tailoring requires organizational permission and support — governance bodies, PMOs, and sponsors enable or constrain the practitioner's ability to tailor (§3.7).

## Questions to Ask in Practice
1. What are the key contextual factors (size, complexity, risk, regulatory constraints, team maturity) that should shape the project approach — and how have they been documented?
2. Is the current level of process and governance documentation proportionate to the project's actual complexity and strategic significance, or are we applying more formality than the situation warrants?
3. Have we reviewed the delivery approach (predictive, hybrid, agile) against the nature of requirements — are requirements stable and known, or do they need iterative discovery?
4. What is the organization's governance appetite, and have we obtained explicit permission to tailor standard processes where deviation is warranted?
5. When project conditions change significantly, do we have a process to revisit and update the tailored approach, rather than continuing to execute a plan that no longer fits?
