# P08 — Build Quality into Processes and Deliverables
**Type:** Principle
**PMBOK8 Source:** PMBOK8 Standard §3.8 — Build Quality into Processes and Deliverables
**Version:** 1.0.0

## Definition
PMBOK 8 Standard §3.8 defines quality in project management across two dimensions: conformance to requirements (the deliverable meets agreed specifications) and fitness for use (the deliverable satisfies the customer's actual need). Quality is not an inspection step applied at the end of delivery — it is built into the processes, practices, and standards that produce deliverables throughout the project lifecycle. Prevention is less costly than correction, and early defect detection is less costly than late-stage rework. The quality principle applies equally to the product being produced and to the project management processes themselves. Delivering a product that meets its specification but was produced through dysfunctional processes is not a quality outcome.

## Key Characteristics
- **Prevention over inspection:** Effort invested in quality planning and quality assurance activities prevents defects from occurring rather than catching them after the fact — reducing the total cost of quality (§3.8).
- **Dual application — product and process:** Quality standards apply both to what the project produces (deliverables, products, services, results) and to how the project is managed (processes, practices, governance) (§3.8).
- **Requirements as quality criteria:** Quality is defined relative to agreed requirements and acceptance criteria — quality management begins with clear, traceable requirements against which conformance can be measured (§3.8).
- **Cost of quality awareness:** Quality decisions involve trade-offs between the cost of conformance (prevention and appraisal activities) and the cost of non-conformance (internal and external failure costs) (§3.8).
- **Continuous improvement:** Quality outcomes feed back into process improvement — retrospectives, lessons learned, and root cause analysis are quality practices that improve future performance (§3.8).
- **Stakeholder satisfaction as a quality measure:** Meeting specifications is necessary but not sufficient; customer satisfaction — whether the delivered result actually serves the intended purpose — is the ultimate quality test (§3.8).

## Questions to Ask in Practice
1. Have we defined quality criteria — in measurable, verifiable terms — for every major deliverable, and have those criteria been agreed with the relevant stakeholders?
2. What quality assurance activities are built into our delivery process to prevent defects from reaching later stages, rather than relying entirely on end-of-phase inspection?
3. Are we tracking the cost of quality — both the cost of prevention/appraisal activities and the cost of defects found — to inform future quality investment decisions?
4. Do our quality processes apply equally to how the project is managed (governance, documentation, process adherence) as well as to what the project produces?
5. When defects or non-conformances occur, do we perform root cause analysis and feed the lessons back into improved processes, or do we simply fix and move on?
