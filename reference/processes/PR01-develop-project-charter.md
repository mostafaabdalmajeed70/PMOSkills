# PR01 — Develop Project Charter
**Type:** Process
**Process Group:** Initiating
**Knowledge Area:** Project Integration Management
**PMBOK8 Source:** Process Groups: A Practice Guide § 2.1
**Version:** 1.0.0

## Definition
Develop Project Charter is the process of developing a document that formally authorizes the existence of a project and provides the project manager with the authority to apply organizational resources to project activities (PG:APG § 2.1). It establishes a direct link between the project and the strategic objectives of the organization, and documents the initial requirements, assumptions, constraints, and high-level project description. The Project Charter is the foundational governance document of every project; without it, the project lacks formal organizational sanction.

## Purpose
To formally authorize the project, define the project manager’s authority level, and document high-level scope, schedule, cost parameters, and stakeholder expectations so that the project begins with a clear organizational mandate.

## Key Tools and Techniques
| Tool / Technique | Description |
|---|---|
| Expert judgment | Assessing information needed to develop the charter from subject matter experts |
| Data gathering (brainstorming, focus groups, interviews) | Collecting inputs from sponsors, stakeholders, and domain experts |
| Interpersonal and team skills (conflict management, facilitation, meeting management) | Building alignment among sponsors and key stakeholders |
| Meetings | Kick-off and charter development sessions with sponsor and key stakeholders |

## Key Outputs
| Output | Description |
|---|---|
| Project Charter | Formal document authorizing the project and granting the PM authority |
| Assumption Log | Initial list of assumptions identified during charter development |

## Key Characteristics
- **Authorization instrument:** The charter is the only document that formally authorizes the project to exist within the organization (PG:APG § 2.1).
- **PM authority grant:** It defines the project manager’s authority to commit resources, make decisions, and engage stakeholders (PG:APG § 2.1).
- **Strategic link:** The charter explicitly connects the project to its strategic business objective or organizational need, preventing scope drift at the outset (PG:APG § 2.1).
- **High-level scope:** It documents high-level requirements, milestones, budget ranges, and constraints — not detailed plans, which come later in planning (PG:APG § 2.1).
- **Issued by sponsor:** The charter is formally issued by the sponsoring entity — not by the project manager — underscoring that authorization comes from outside the project (PG:APG § 2.1).

## Questions to Ask in Practice
1. Has the sponsoring executive formally issued the charter, or has the project manager drafted it themselves without sponsor endorsement — if so, is it truly authorized?
2. Does the charter explicitly link the project to a named strategic objective in the organization’s portfolio or program plan?
3. Are the high-level success criteria in the charter measurable, or are they vague statements that will be disputed at closure?
4. What assumptions have been embedded in the charter that have not yet been validated — and are they recorded in the Assumption Log?
5. Does the charter clearly define what is out of scope, preventing scope creep before planning begins?
