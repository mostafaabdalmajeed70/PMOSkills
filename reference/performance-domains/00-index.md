# Performance Domains — Reference Index
**Type:** Index
**PMBOK8 Source:** PMBOK8 Guide Part 2 §2.1–§2.8
**Version:** 1.0.0

## Purpose

Performance domains are the eight concurrent areas of attention that define what project managers and project teams must address throughout the entire project lifecycle. PMBOK 8 introduces performance domains as the organizing structure for its guide (Part 2), replacing the process group and knowledge area matrix that structured earlier editions.

**Performance domains are not sequential phases.** They are concurrent areas of focus that overlap and interact throughout the project from initiation through closure. At any point in a project, a practitioner may need to simultaneously attend to stakeholder engagement, planning, team development, delivery quality, and risk — all at once. The domains do not prescribe when to perform activities; they describe what categories of work must be continuously attended to.

**How performance domains differ from process groups:** Process groups (Initiating, Planning, Executing, Monitoring and Controlling, Closing) describe a temporal sequence of activity. Performance domains describe thematic areas of ongoing attention. The *Process Groups: A Practice Guide* provides the process-level detail that complements these domain descriptions; the PMBOK 8 Guide uses performance domains to communicate intent, principles, and outcomes rather than prescriptive procedures.

**Why PMBOK 8 uses domains:** The shift to performance domains reflects PMI's recognition that project management practice has diversified across predictive, agile, and hybrid approaches. Domains describe what matters — the outcomes to achieve — without prescribing how to achieve them. This allows practitioners to tailor their approach while remaining grounded in a common framework of professional intent.

## All 8 Performance Domains

| ID | Domain Name | PMBOK8 Source | Brief Description |
|---|---|---|---|
| PD01 | Stakeholders | PMBOK8 §2.1 | Addresses activities associated with stakeholders — identifying who is affected by the project, understanding their interests and influence, engaging them appropriately, and maintaining productive relationships throughout the project lifecycle. |
| PD02 | Team | PMBOK8 §2.2 | Addresses activities associated with establishing and maintaining a high-performing project team — shared leadership, collaboration, psychological safety, team development, and the conditions that enable effective collective work. |
| PD03 | Development Approach and Life Cycle | PMBOK8 §2.3 | Addresses the selection and management of the project development approach (predictive, agile, or hybrid), the project life cycle structure, and the phasing and cadences through which work and deliverables are organized. |
| PD04 | Planning | PMBOK8 §2.4 | Addresses activities associated with organizing and coordinating project work — establishing baselines for scope, schedule, cost, quality, and risk; maintaining plans as living documents; and adapting plans as new information emerges. |
| PD05 | Project Work | PMBOK8 §2.5 | Addresses activities associated with establishing project processes, managing physical resources, managing procurement, maintaining communications, and fostering a learning environment — the operational management of how the project runs day-to-day. |
| PD06 | Delivery | PMBOK8 §2.6 | Addresses activities associated with delivering the scope and quality the project was undertaken to achieve — requirements management, scope control, quality management, stakeholder acceptance, and the distinction between outputs, outcomes, and benefits. |
| PD07 | Measurement | PMBOK8 §2.7 | Addresses activities associated with assessing project performance and taking corrective action — establishing a meaningful measurement system, analyzing performance against baselines, reporting to stakeholders, and guarding against measurement dysfunction. |
| PD08 | Uncertainty | PMBOK8 §2.8 | Addresses activities associated with risk and uncertainty — including ambiguity, complexity, volatility, and risk — through systematic identification, analysis, and response to both threats and opportunities across the project lifecycle. |

**Key interaction notes:**
- **PD04 Planning** is the most widely connected domain — it receives inputs from and sends outputs to all other domains; plans are the integration point of the project
- **PD05 Project Work** and **PD06 Delivery** are tightly coupled — work processes produce deliverables; delivery requirements shape work execution
- **PD07 Measurement** and **PD05 Project Work** are mutually dependent — work generates performance data; measurement analysis drives corrective work actions
- **PD08 Uncertainty** and **PD07 Measurement** are bidirectional — measurement reveals emerging risks; risk events drive re-measurement and reforecasting
- **PD01 Stakeholders** connects to **PD06 Delivery** — deliverables are ultimately accepted by stakeholders; acceptance drives both delivery completion and stakeholder satisfaction

## Key Concepts Across Domains

**Tailoring:** No domain prescribes a fixed set of activities. Each domain's activities are tailored to project size, complexity, development approach, organizational context, and stakeholder needs. A small agile project applies all eight domains but with lighter-weight, more iterative practices than a large infrastructure program.

**Concurrent attention:** Project managers do not "finish" one domain and move to the next. Stakeholder engagement, planning, measurement, and uncertainty management all operate simultaneously. The domains are lenses, not phases.

**Outcomes over outputs:** PMBOK 8 consistently emphasizes outcomes (changes in state or behavior) and benefits (value realized from outcomes) over outputs (deliverables produced). Each domain should be evaluated by whether it is contributing to the intended outcomes of the project, not merely completing its prescribed activities.

**Integration:** The domains interact through shared artifacts, shared team members, and shared information flows. The Project Manager's role is to maintain coherent integration across all eight domains simultaneously — this is the integrative challenge that performance domain thinking is designed to highlight.
